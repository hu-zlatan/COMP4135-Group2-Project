\chapter{Introduction}

\projectname{} is the final Unity coursework project developed by Group 2 for the COMP4135 Software Engineering Management module. The project answers the CW1 brief by producing a working game suitable for teenagers while also demonstrating requirements engineering, implementation planning, validation, documentation, and team-based software delivery \cite{cw1Specification,finalReportTemplate}. Rather than attempting a content-heavy RPG or a large collectible card game, the team chose a compact tactical card battle that could be implemented, tested, and refined within the available coursework window.

\section{Motivation}

The team's starting motivation was to build a game that encouraged planning, positioning, and short-horizon tactical decision making without overwhelming the player with large maps, progression systems, or complex rules. This aligned well with the coursework requirement to produce a game for teenagers: the design could stay strategically meaningful while remaining readable, approachable, and demonstrable in a short play session \cite{onePager,cw1Specification}.

The tactical card format was chosen because it combined two strengths. First, a grid-based battle creates visible positional decisions that are easy to explain and verify. Second, a limited hand of command cards introduces turn-by-turn choice without requiring a large content set. This combination allowed the team to aim for a game that felt deliberate rather than random, while still keeping the scope realistic for a small student team.

\section{Aims and Objectives}

The project had four main objectives.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Deliver a complete playable battle loop in Unity, including player turn, enemy turn, and battle end state.
    \item Keep the gameplay small enough to be implemented and validated properly within coursework scope.
    \item Build the game in a way that leaves clear evidence of engineering practice, including requirements traceability, testing records, and version-control workflow.
    \item Improve readability and feedback so a first-time player can understand what to do without external explanation.
\end{enumerate}

Success was therefore defined less by content quantity than by the completeness and quality of one polished vertical slice. A successful outcome meant that the player could enter the game, start a battle, understand the core interaction model, finish the encounter, and see the result in a stable and test-backed system.

\section{Description of the Work}

The final system is a compact tactical battle demo built around one encounter scene. The player begins from a title screen, starts a battle, controls two allied units on a small grid, uses a hand of tactical cards to move, attack, defend, reposition, or recover, and then ends the turn so enemy units can act. The battle finishes in a clear victory or defeat state and can then be replayed or exited back to the menu.

Over time, the project evolved from a prototype-focused slice into a more complete coursework deliverable. WR1 established the concept and initial requirements. WR2 locked down the main implementation decisions, including the single-battle vertical slice and explicit, readable battle logic. WR3 concentrated on test strategy and execution evidence. After WR3, the team extended the front-end flow, HUD feedback, UI theming, and battle readability so the final project would feel complete enough for final submission rather than only technically functional.

The result is intentionally modest in content, but it is coherent in scope: one polished encounter, one maintainable codebase, one testable battle loop, and one reportable development trail.
