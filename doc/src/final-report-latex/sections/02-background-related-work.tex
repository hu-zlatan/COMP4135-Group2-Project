\chapter{Background and Related Work}

This chapter situates the game within a small-scale tactical design space and explains why that design direction is suitable for the intended audience. The focus remains practical: the aim is to justify the core interaction model rather than to provide a broad survey of commercial tactics games.

\section{Design Context}

The delivered game sits between a small-scale tactical skirmish game and a card-driven command system. It is not intended to compete with content-heavy strategy RPGs or large collectible card games. Instead, it adopts the parts of those genres that support clear turn structure, visible positional play, and short decision cycles.

The core design context is a lightweight tactical grid battle in which the player controls a very small team and spends a limited hand of cards each turn. Positioning, range, targeting, and action ordering are the main decision variables, while content breadth is intentionally constrained. The project therefore uses cards as a readable action language, not as a collectible system with large pools, rarity, or synergy chains.

Unity was selected because the game depends on both world-space interaction and screen-space feedback. The board, tile highlights, unit placement, and combat feedback all live in the same runtime environment as the HUD, menu flow, and result presentation. That makes Unity a better fit than a document-only prototype or a purely web-first interaction model.

\section{Audience Fit}

The target group is teenage and casual strategy players. First, the game is structured around short repeated decisions rather than long uninterrupted sessions. This fits the idea that games can support competence and agency when the player is given clear choices and immediate consequences \cite{ryan2006motivationalpull}. Each turn in \projectname{} asks the player to choose one unit, one card, and one legal target, which keeps the decision loop visible instead of overwhelming.

Second, the strategic layer remains compact but genuine. Adachi and Willoughby report a positive longitudinal relationship between strategic video-game play and self-reported problem-solving skills among adolescents \cite{adachi2013strategicgames}. \projectname{} does not claim to be an educational intervention in the strict research sense, but it does adopt the same design intuition: ask the player to reason about position, range, and action order under bounded resources.

Third, the project intentionally avoids features that would weaken its fit for the target audience in a compact prototype. There is no large narrative gate, no deckbuilding complexity, no rarity economy, and no large campaign state to memorise. Instead, the player sees a small board, a limited hand, explicit prompt text, and direct feedback after each action. Granic et al. note that the benefits of games are often tied to how clearly challenge, mastery, and feedback are structured \cite{granic2014benefits}; the final product therefore prioritises readable battle flow over content breadth.

\section{Reference Strategy}

The project uses external references selectively and at two different levels. At the gameplay level, it borrows only broad genre patterns: grid occupancy, ranged versus melee positioning, and card-labelled actions. No external gameplay framework is imported into the runtime. The battle loop, flow coordination, grid logic, enemy response, and HUD behaviour are authored within the project codebase.

At the visual level, the interface uses open-licensed panel and icon resources, while the board dressing, layout logic, and gameplay-facing presentation behaviour remain locally implemented. The visual surface is therefore themed, but the runtime interaction design is still original to this project.

The related-work boundary remains intentionally narrow. The most relevant comparison points are genre conventions, research-based audience fit, and asset-source choices, not a long survey of published games.
